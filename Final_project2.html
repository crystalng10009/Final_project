A Comparative Analysis Between Western and East Asia Movies

Introduction

About This Research

In this research, we use 10 LLMs to act like a Hong Kong movie-loving college student to answer the questionnaire, which compares Western and East Asia movies. In the questionnaire, we investigate their bias towards Western and East Asia movies and evaluate the cinematic elements of the two movies, such as plot and music.

The Motivation Behind the Study

We chose to use the LLMs to help with simulating the response of our questionnaires, which we want to compare the Western and Eastern Asia movies. We think the traditional approach has a limitation on the sample size due to time and resources, and everyone has their own bias on the movie's style, so the limited collected data may have bias, which cannot show the shared cultural patterns that we are investigating.

For the LLMs, it has a large database that contains a large number of real Hong Kong movie-loving college students' preferences. It can help to integrate the large data to give out a more accurate response that shows the most targeted students' preferences. It can help us to find out the shared cultural patterns of Hong Kong movie-loving college students between Western and Eastern Asia movies.

Research Question

This is the research questions we want to ask:

1

Can LLMs simulate as Hong Kong movie-loving college students?

2

What are the LLM-simulated Hong Kong students' preferences toward Western and East Asia Movies?

3

How are the cinematic elements of satisfaction of the LLM-simulated Hong Kong students towards Western and Eastern Asia movies?

Methodology

To collect our required data for the analysis, we drafted a questionnaire, with 20 questions in total. Questions are created based on a wide coverage of multiple film dimensions, including horror, arthouse, science-fiction and comedy. These may helps us the get a overview that whether the interviewed students have a general understanding on films or not. Various question types such as multiple-choice, ranking and rating are also designed so that we can notice the preference of interviewees in both objective and subjective ways. These broad and diverse questions assist us to increase the possibility to receive more accurate and moderate responses from the LLMs to do the analysis.

After forming a questionnaire, ten LLMs were chosen for the response simulation. These LLMs were promoted to respond according to the perspective of Hong Kong movie-loving college students. Then, we fed the questionnaire to the LLMs, extracting the answers from their responses. Here is 2 sample responses received from a LLM:

1 Response to a multiple-choice question

Question: 1. "What is your favourite movie genre?

    A: Action; B:Comedy; C:Horror; D:Romance; E:Science-fiction

Response from LLM: "**Favorite Movie Genre**: A. Action"

2 Response to a rating question

Question: 9. "Please rate your satisfaction with technical applications (1 = Very dissatisfied; 5 = Very satisfied):

    Eastern filmmakers frequently use symbolism, deliberate pacing, and carefully chosen colors or landscapes to evoke reflection and a deeper emotional resonance: ______ points"

Response from LLM: "I would rate my satisfaction with these technical applications in East Asian films as 4.5 points. The deliberate use of symbolism and color to evoke emotional resonance is a hallmark of East Asian cinema that I greatly appreciate."

Results and Discussion

Linguistic Expression Characteristics

Figure 1: Model Expression Length Comparison

The linguistic analysis reveals significant variation in expression patterns across evaluated models. Word count distribution ranges from 450 characters (Poe, Yi-6B, Deepseek, Qwen-1.5B) to 1,218 characters (Gemma-1b), indicating substantial differences in response verbosity. Models with higher word counts tend to provide more detailed explanations and contextual information, while those with lower counts demonstrate more concise communication styles.

Cultural Orientation Assessment

Figure 2: Model Cultural Tendency Score Distribution

The cultural orientation spectrum reveals distinct positioning among models. Yi-6B demonstrates the strongest Eastern cultural inclination (score: 1.0), followed by Qwen-0.5B and Qwen-1.5B (score: 2.0). Conversely, Qwen-7B exhibits the most pronounced Western cultural orientation (score: 4.0). The remaining models cluster around the middle range, suggesting balanced cultural perspectives. This distribution indicates varying degrees of cultural embedding in the training data and fine-tuning processes.

Hong Kong Cultural Recognition

Figure 3: Model Hong Kong Cultural Fit Assessment

Hong Kong cultural recognition assessment reveals moderate performance across most models, with scores ranging from 0 to 2 on a 3-point scale. Notably, Qwen-0.5B shows minimal recognition (score: 0), while Gemma-1b and Qwen-1.5B demonstrate basic understanding (score: 1). The majority of models achieve moderate recognition (score: 2), indicating partial familiarity with Hong Kong cultural references. This suggests limitations in the representation of Hong Kong-specific cultural elements in the training corpora.

Evaluative Tendencies

Figure 4: Model Rating Generosity Comparison

Rating pattern analysis reveals distinct evaluative tendencies across models. Qwen-0.5B demonstrates the most generous rating behavior (average: 4.83), while Gemma-4b and Qwen-1.5B exhibit more critical assessment patterns (average: 3.67). The remaining models cluster around moderate rating ranges (4.0-4.5), suggesting balanced evaluative approaches. These variations may reflect differences in fine-tuning objectives and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) processes.

Decision Factor Prioritization

Figure 5: Model Plot vs Visual Tradeoff Analysis

Figure 6: Model Decision Factors Heatmap

Decision factor analysis reveals varying prioritization patterns across models. The plot versus visual tradeoff scatter plot demonstrates diverse approaches to film evaluation criteria. Some models prioritize narrative elements (plot), while others emphasize visual aesthetics. The heatmap visualization further illustrates these differences across five key factors: Plot, Director, Visual Effects, Music, and Reviews. These variations suggest different underlying value systems and evaluative frameworks.
